## 1. Introduction

Electrical schematics are compact visual representations of functional structure, connectivity, and design intent. Converting such diagrams into machine-usable artefacts is therefore an important engineering problem rather than a narrow image-recognition task. A successful system must recover topology, component relationships, and enough structural detail to support downstream analysis, simulation, or redesign. In practice, however, schematic interpretation often remains manual or semi-manual, especially when the source material is an image, scan, or exported diagram rather than a native project file. This creates friction across engineering workflows that depend on translation between visual documentation and executable design representations.

The broader engineering-software landscape provides part of the motivation for this problem. As the current Related Work synthesis in this repository shows, AI capability in engineering tools is advancing rapidly in selected domains such as PCB layout optimisation, multiphysics simulation, and copilot-style assistance within large design platforms. At the same time, the same synthesis identifies a persistent gap in structural circuit recovery: existing tools and workflows are much stronger at accelerating established expert activity than at reconstructing simulator-ready circuit structure from heterogeneous visual inputs. The reports also highlight the growing relevance of multimodal and agent-based systems, but they note that these systems are rarely evaluated through a common workflow that preserves engineering artefacts, supports cross-model comparison, and treats validation as part of the experiment rather than as an afterthought.

This gap is especially important for schematic understanding because useful recovery requires more than a textual description of what a diagram may depict. An engineering-useful reconstruction must support downstream artefacts such as interactive circuit views, simulator-oriented schematic files, and structured reports of assumptions, inconsistencies, and missing information. Existing commercial and adjacent research workflows provide only limited support for this kind of validation-aware, multi-representation recovery, and systematic comparison of modern agent systems on this task remains sparse. As a result, it is still unclear how reliably current multimodal coding and reasoning agents can convert schematic images into structurally meaningful circuit representations.

To address this problem, this paper studies schematic reconstruction as a multi-agent benchmarking task. The proposed workflow evaluates Codex, Claude, and Gemini under a common orchestration framework and requires each system to generate two complementary downstream modalities from an input schematic image: a Falstad-compatible circuit representation and an LTSpice schematic representation. The benchmark further incorporates automated validation, issue reporting, and rendered artefact inspection so that comparison is based not only on whether output files were produced, but on whether those files yield recognizable, inspectable, and potentially simulator-usable circuit structure. In this way, the study treats schematic understanding as a reconstruction pipeline rather than a one-shot captioning or diagram-description exercise.

The contribution of the paper is threefold. First, it defines a practical benchmarking workflow for evaluating multiple agent systems on structural circuit reconstruction from schematic images. Second, it operationalises this evaluation through dual-modality output generation, combining Falstad and LTSpice artefacts with validation reports and preview recovery inside a web-based orchestration pipeline. Third, it uses this framework to analyse success and failure behaviour across circuits of varying complexity, thereby exposing both the promise and the brittleness of current agent-based approaches to engineering diagram understanding.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section II reviews the engineering-software AI landscape and the gap motivating validation-aware schematic reconstruction. Section III describes the multi-agent orchestration pipeline, dual-modality artefact generation, and validation procedures. Section IV presents the empirical results across the benchmark circuits, and Section V discusses the implications, limitations, and future directions suggested by the observed success and failure modes.

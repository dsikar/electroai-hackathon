## 2. Related Work

Recent survey and gap-analysis work in this repository shows that AI in engineering software is no longer absent, but it remains unevenly distributed across domains and workflow stages. The strongest built-in capabilities appear in high-value areas such as PCB layout optimisation, multiphysics simulation, and broad engineering-platform assistance. Cadence, Siemens NX, Ansys, and MATLAB/Simulink are repeatedly identified as relatively mature examples, with shipping support for optimisation, copilot-style interaction, and simulation-oriented acceleration. By contrast, schematic-centric, industrial electrical, and fluid-power environments remain much more dependent on rule-based automation, macros, templates, and external assistants. This asymmetry is important for circuit-understanding research because it suggests that current commercial progress has focused more on accelerating expert use within established tools than on recovering structured representations from heterogeneous engineering artefacts.

Within electronics-focused tooling, the repository surveys identify Proteus EDAi as a notable example of deeper AI integration, because it couples language-model interaction to live schematic, simulation, and firmware context rather than offering only generic text assistance. However, the same reports show that this level of context-aware support is unusual. In Altium, EPLAN, and several adjacent platforms, the boundary between genuine AI and advanced automation remains blurred, and the surveyed reports repeatedly note disagreement between vendor framing, surrounding cloud services, and what is clearly available in the core design workflow. The gap analyses therefore emphasise a methodological distinction between AI that is grounded in engineering state and AI that merely wraps documentation, search, or workflow convenience around existing software.

These reports also position schematic and circuit interpretation as an adjacent but underdeveloped area. Across the surveyed landscape, practical opportunities are repeatedly framed in terms of reading schematics, wiring diagrams, hydraulic circuits, or engineering PDFs and converting them into structured artefacts such as component lists, connectivity descriptions, validation outputs, bills of materials, or simulator configurations. In other words, the need is not simply for another conversational copilot, but for systems that can recover engineering structure from visual or semi-structured inputs. The root-level gap analyses are explicit that current tools provide little direct support for topology-preserving structural recovery, especially when the input is an image or exported diagram rather than a native editable project.

The survey materials further suggest that multimodal and agent-based workflows are presently more important in practice than monolithic built-in AI. External language models are already being used around engineering packages for code generation, documentation, explanation, and workflow automation, while newer interfaces such as MCP make tool-using orchestration more tractable across otherwise disconnected environments. This is especially relevant to schematic understanding, where a complete workflow may require image interpretation, structured reconstruction, simulator-oriented export, validation, and rendered preview generation rather than a single inference step. Yet the surveyed products do not provide a common experimental interface for comparing multiple agent systems on the same circuit-recovery task, nor do they generally preserve per-model outputs in a way that supports reproducible cross-model benchmarking.

The gap identified across the reports is therefore broader than the absence of AI in any single package. Existing tools remain weak in structural fidelity, in preservation of circuit topology across representations, in generation of directly simulator-ready artefacts, and in validation of AI-generated engineering outputs. Cross-tool intelligence is also notably underdeveloped: the gap analyses highlight the lack of systems that can move reliably from one engineering representation to another while retaining intent, checking consistency, and surfacing likely errors. Even where generation has improved, verification and review remain largely manual. This leaves a clear methodological need for workflows that treat reconstruction quality, artefact completeness, and downstream usability as first-class evaluation targets rather than as by-products of a copilot interaction.

The present study is positioned in that gap. Rather than evaluating a single proprietary assistant inside one engineering platform, it benchmarks multiple agent systems on a shared schematic-to-structure task and requires each system to produce dual downstream modalities: Falstad circuit representations and LTSpice schematic artefacts. The workflow also incorporates validation reports and visual preview recovery so that comparison is based not only on whether text was generated, but on whether recognisable, inspectable, and simulator-oriented circuit structure was recovered. In this sense, the contribution of the project is not the introduction of another generic engineering copilot, but a practical multi-agent benchmarking pipeline focused specifically on structural circuit reconstruction from schematic images.

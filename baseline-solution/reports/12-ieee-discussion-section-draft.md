## 5. Discussion

The results indicate that the multi-agent pipeline can recover some circuit structure from schematic images, but that this ability remains conditional, fragile, and strongly dependent on circuit complexity. The clearest success mode is partial structural recovery on simpler circuits. In the first four benchmark items, at least one agent, and usually Claude, was able to generate Falstad and LTSpice artefacts that preserved a recognizable topology even when component values, operating conditions, or exact net-level commitments remained uncertain. This matters because it shows that current multimodal and coding agents are not limited to generic visual description; under favorable conditions they can produce simulator-oriented outputs that are sufficiently structured to support visual inspection and downstream engineering review.

At the same time, the early successes are best interpreted as constrained rather than robust. Even the stronger reconstructions were accompanied by validation findings, missing values, assumed reference conditions, or mismatches between the textual analysis and the rendered LTSpice structure. In other words, the pipeline can often recover the broad organizational pattern of a circuit before it can recover a trustworthy engineering instantiation of that circuit. This distinction is important. For practical use, a recognizable topology is useful for triage, explanation, or educational assistance, but it is not equivalent to a verified simulator-ready reconstruction.

The failure modes are equally informative. The later circuits show that degradation is not only quantitative but qualitative: outputs do not merely become somewhat less accurate, but often collapse into sparse Falstad netlists, placeholder LTSpice schematics, or internally inconsistent artefact sets. This suggests that the limiting factor is not only symbol recognition, but the ability to maintain a coherent internal representation of component identity, connectivity, and cross-modal consistency as the circuit becomes less canonical or more structurally demanding. The observed failures therefore support the view that circuit reconstruction is a topology-recovery task rather than a straightforward image-captioning problem.

The results also suggest a complexity trend. Simpler and more canonical circuits appear to be handled more reliably, while the later benchmark items expose brittleness in structural recovery. The transition is not perfectly abrupt for every agent or every modality, but the benchmark does show a practical threshold beyond which stable dual-modality reconstruction is no longer sustained. This is consistent with the methodological design of the study: once a model must preserve multiple relationships simultaneously across two target representations, local visual plausibility is no longer enough. Later failures therefore reveal a structural capacity limit rather than a small accumulation of isolated mistakes.

Differences between agents are visible but should be interpreted carefully. Claude appears to be the strongest system in this benchmark because it most consistently produces Falstad and LTSpice artefacts that remain visually interpretable across the full set. Codex occupies an intermediate position: some Falstad outputs preserve meaningful structure, but the LTSpice branch is less stable and more often reduced to placeholder or incomplete output. Gemini is weakest at the artefact level, because plausible report prose is repeatedly accompanied by low-information Falstad output or placeholder LTSpice files. This pattern suggests that linguistic plausibility and engineering reconstruction quality can diverge substantially, which further justifies the use of rendered artefacts and validation rather than relying on text summaries alone.

The dual-modality design of the pipeline is therefore methodologically useful rather than redundant. Falstad previews are more forgiving and are often the first modality to reveal whether some topological information has been preserved. LTSpice previews, by contrast, are stricter and make malformed or placeholder outputs more obvious. The two modalities thus expose different aspects of model behaviour: Falstad is useful for rapid structural inspection, while LTSpice is a stronger test of whether that structure has been committed in a form closer to a conventional engineering workflow. The benchmark results imply that multi-modal reconstruction is meaningfully harder than producing a textual explanation, and that simulator usability and apparent structural plausibility do not necessarily coincide.

These findings also support the broader methodological choices of the project. Preserving per-agent outputs, validating required artefacts, and rendering generated outputs for inspection all proved necessary for meaningful comparison. If the study had accepted generated files at face value, several weak or placeholder outputs would have been misclassified as successful runs. The orchestration layer is therefore part of the contribution: it converts model responses into a comparable experimental substrate and makes failure modes observable rather than implicit. In that sense, the study addresses a gap identified in the related-work analysis, namely the absence of practical workflows that benchmark multiple agent systems on structural engineering reconstruction tasks while preserving downstream artefacts for review.

The study nevertheless has important limitations. The benchmark set is small, the circuit classes are limited, and the complexity progression is only lightly sampled. Performance may also be sensitive to image quality, schematic style, missing labels, and the particular prompting or model versions used in the runners. In addition, the current validation layer focuses on artefact completeness, report content, and broad structural plausibility rather than full functional equivalence. A rendered Falstad or LTSpice preview is therefore best treated as evidence of partial structural recovery, not proof that the recovered circuit would behave identically to the source design under simulation.

Future work should strengthen both evaluation and reconstruction. On the evaluation side, the next step is a richer benchmark with more circuits, broader schematic styles, and explicit ground-truth topology so that component recall, connection accuracy, and cross-modal consistency can be measured more directly. On the reconstruction side, the pipeline would benefit from stronger topology-aware prompting, automatic netlist checking, graph-based comparison against reference circuits, and tighter coupling between image interpretation and downstream simulator constraints. It would also be valuable to test whether tool-augmented agents can iteratively repair weak reconstructions instead of producing a single one-shot output. More broadly, the results suggest that progress in this area will depend less on generic multimodal fluency than on methods that explicitly model engineering structure and verify it across representations.

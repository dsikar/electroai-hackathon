## 6. Conclusion

This paper examined whether current multimodal and agent-based AI systems can recover engineering-useful circuit representations from schematic images under a common reconstruction workflow. Using a shared orchestration pipeline, Codex, Claude, and Gemini were evaluated on the generation of two downstream modalities, Falstad and LTSpice, together with validation-aware reporting and rendered artefact inspection.

The results show that current performance is mixed. Simpler benchmark circuits were at least partially recoverable, and the strongest runs produced recognisable circuit structure in one or both output modalities. At the same time, more demanding circuits exposed clear failure modes, including sparse Falstad outputs, placeholder LTSpice artefacts, and validation findings indicating incomplete or unstable structural recovery. Agent behaviour also differed in quality and consistency, with Claude producing the strongest overall artefacts, Codex preserving some structure but with weaker LTSpice stability, and Gemini more frequently collapsing to low-information outputs.

These findings highlight both the value and the present limits of the approach. The study shows that a multi-agent, validation-aware pipeline can provide a useful experimental framework for comparing schematic reconstruction behaviour and for surfacing engineering artefacts that are more informative than text-only responses. The complementary use of Falstad and LTSpice also proved important, since the two modalities reveal different aspects of structural plausibility and failure. However, the benchmark equally demonstrates that current models do not yet provide reliable topology-preserving reconstruction across increasing circuit complexity, and that rendered outputs must be interpreted cautiously unless supported by stronger structural verification.

The broader significance of the work is therefore methodological as much as empirical. Rather than treating schematic understanding as a generic vision-language task, the paper frames it as an engineering reconstruction problem that requires artefact preservation, cross-model comparison, and explicit validation. This perspective is relevant to future AI-assisted engineering workflows in which useful automation depends not only on descriptive fluency, but on the ability to produce inspectable, simulator-oriented, and internally consistent technical outputs.

Future work should extend the benchmark to larger and more diverse schematic datasets, introduce stronger topology-aware and netlist-level evaluation metrics, and incorporate functional verification through simulation or equivalence checking. Additional gains may also come from improved prompting, tool-augmented repair loops, and tighter integration between image interpretation and downstream engineering constraints. Taken together, the present results suggest that AI agents can sometimes recover meaningful circuit structure from images, but that dependable schematic reconstruction will require more explicit modelling and verification of engineering structure than current general-purpose systems yet provide.

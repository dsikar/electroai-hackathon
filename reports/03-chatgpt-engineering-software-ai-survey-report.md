## Executive Summary

Across mainstream engineering software, **AI integration is still uneven and domain-dependent**. High-end EDA and systems platforms (notably from Siemens, Autodesk, and Cadence Design Systems) have begun embedding **AI-driven optimisation, generative design, and verification**, particularly in chip design and complex systems engineering.

However, in **PCB design, schematic capture, and fluid power (hydraulic/pneumatic) tools**, AI is still **largely external**, with workflows relying on scripting, rule-based automation, or third-party AI copilots.

This creates a clear opportunity: **AI agents that sit on top of engineering tools—interpreting schematics, generating designs, or automating workflows—are still largely unexplored in practice**, making them highly suitable for the EEAI Hackathon.

# Observations

### 1. Where AI is genuinely built-in (highest → lowest)

1. **IC design & simulation** (Cadence, ANSYS)
2. **Systems & lifecycle platforms** (Siemens NX)
3. **General modelling platforms** (MATLAB)
4. **Mechanical CAD** (Fusion 360)
5. **PCB & electrical tools** (largely lagging)
6. **Hydraulic/pneumatic tools** (almost none)

---

### 2. Automation vs AI

* Many tools market **“automation” as AI**, but:

  * rule-based routing ≠ AI
  * macro reuse ≠ AI
* True AI appears where:

  * optimisation is non-deterministic
  * ML models are trained (e.g., Cadence Cerebrus)

---

### 3. External AI dominates practice

Common real workflows:

* LLMs explaining schematics
* AI generating code (Arduino, HDL)
* AI parsing datasheets
* AI assisting documentation

→ **Most innovation is happening outside the tools**

---

### 4. Underserved domains

* Hydraulic systems
* Pneumatic systems
* PCB-level intelligent design
* Industrial electrical schematics

These are prime areas for hackathon exploration.

---

# Hackathon Relevance (EEAI)

## Key Insight

The gap is not “better AI inside tools” —
it is **AI agents that understand and operate engineering tools externally**.

---

## High-Value Hackathon Directions

### 1. AI schematic understanding agents

* Input: schematic (image or file)
* Output:

  * explanation
  * fault detection
  * simulation suggestions

---

### 2. AI co-pilot for PCB design

* Suggest routing improvements
* Auto-generate layouts from constraints
* Validate design rules conversationally

---

### 3. Fluid system AI assistants (high gap)

* Generate hydraulic/pneumatic circuits from specs
* Diagnose faults in simulation models
* Optimise energy efficiency

---

### 4. Cross-domain AI workflows

* Connect:

  * MATLAB models
  * EDA tools
  * simulation outputs

→ unified reasoning agent

---

### 5. Documentation & reverse engineering

* Convert:

  * schematics → text
  * simulation → reports
* Auto-generate lab reports or design justifications

---

## Final Positioning

The survey shows:

* **AI inside engineering tools = emerging but uneven**
* **AI around engineering tools = wide open opportunity**

For the EEAI Hackathon, the strongest framing is:

> “Can AI agents understand, reason about, and act on engineering artefacts (schematics, simulations, and systems) across tools?”

This aligns directly with:

* vision + language models
* multimodal reasoning
* real engineering workflows

and exposes **clear, unsolved problems suitable for student innovation**.
